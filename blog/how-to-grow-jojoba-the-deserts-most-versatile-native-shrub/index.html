How to Grow Jojoba: The Desert's Most Versatile Native Shrub

June 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by Rino Adamo on Pexels

Jojoba (Simmondsia chinensis) is one of the desert Southwest's most remarkable native plants. Dense enough to serve as a privacy hedge, drought-tolerant enough to survive on three inches of annual rainfall, and productive enough to supply a global cosmetics industry — this evergreen shrub does more than almost any other native in your landscape. If you're looking for a low-maintenance, wildlife-friendly plant that earns its keep, jojoba belongs in your desert garden.

What Is Jojoba?

Despite its species name, jojoba is not from China — Simmondsia chinensis was a 19th-century botanical error. The shrub is native to the Sonoran and Mojave deserts of the American Southwest and Baja California, where it thrives at elevations from sea level to 5,000 feet. It belongs to its own plant family, Simmondsiaceae, making it truly one of a kind.

Jojoba's defining characteristic is the liquid wax found in its seeds — often called jojoba oil, though it's chemically a wax ester rather than a triglyceride oil. This wax is remarkably stable, doesn't oxidize or go rancid, and closely mimics the sebum your skin naturally produces. That's why it appears in hundreds of skincare, haircare, and cosmetic products worldwide.

In the garden, jojoba grows as a dense, rounded to spreading shrub, typically reaching 5 to 8 feet tall and wide. In ideal conditions it can hit 15 feet. It's dioecious — male and female flowers appear on separate plants — so you'll need both sexes within pollination range for females to produce seeds.

Why Grow Jojoba?

The reasons to grow jojoba in a desert garden extend well beyond its commercial value:

  • Wildlife magnet. Gambel's quail, white-winged doves, coyotes, deer, and javelinas all rely on jojoba nuts as a high-fat food source through the lean months of late summer and fall.
  • Privacy and windbreak. Jojoba's thick, year-round evergreen foliage makes it one of the best native options for hedges and screens in the Sonoran Desert. It competes directly with Texas Ranger and jojoba as described in our guide to the best desert plants for privacy screens and hedges.
  • Soil stabilizer. Deep taproots anchor slopes, check erosion, and help jojoba survive on rock-strewn hillsides where few other shrubs persist.
  • Harvest potential. A mature female plant can produce several pounds of seeds annually — enough to supply a household with jojoba wax for personal care use.

Choosing the Right Plants

For home gardeners, the main decision is whether to grow jojoba from seed, cuttings, or nursery transplants.

Transplants are the fastest path to establishment and give you the option to buy sexed specimens — labeled male or female — so you can ensure seed production. Most Sonoran Desert nurseries carry jojoba in one- and five-gallon containers. A healthy gallon plant will establish quickly if you follow our advice on the best time of year to plant in the desert — October through February is the sweet spot for getting roots established before summer heat arrives.

Seeds are inexpensive and germinate readily (soak overnight in warm water, then plant one inch deep in fast-draining cactus mix), but you won't know the sex of your seedlings for several years — until they flower for the first time.

Jojoba is one of the few desert shrubs that rewards patience with genuine productivity. A five-year-old female plant in a Sonoran Desert yard can produce enough seeds to supply a household with jojoba wax for a full year.

For seed production, plan for at least one male plant per three to five females. One male can pollinate females up to half a mile away via wind, but keeping a male within 50 feet maximizes your harvest.

Soil and Planting

Jojoba thrives in almost any well-draining soil — decomposed granite, sandy loam, rocky hillside mix — and tolerates the alkaline pH common throughout the Sonoran and Mojave deserts. What it cannot tolerate is standing water or compacted clay. If your soil drains slowly, amend the planting hole with native sand or crushed granite.

Plant jojoba at the same depth it sat in its container. Dig a hole two to three times as wide as the root ball, backfill with native soil (no compost or amendments for well-draining soil), and water in deeply. Position a drip emitter at the outer edge of the root zone — not directly at the trunk — to encourage outward root growth and reduce the risk of crown rot.

Jojoba is a natural companion to other natives. In its native Sonoran habitat it grows alongside desert ironwood (Olneya tesota), brittlebush (Encelia farinosa), and triangle-leaf bursage (Ambrosia deltoidea). In the home garden, it pairs beautifully with desert willow, ocotillo, and hedgehog cactus. For ideas on building a productive native community, see our guide to desert companion planting.

Watering Jojoba

Once established — typically after two full growing seasons — jojoba needs almost no supplemental water in the Sonoran or Mojave desert. During establishment, water deeply every two weeks through summer, monthly through fall and winter. After that, a few deep soaks through monsoon gaps are usually sufficient.

Overwatering is the most common way to kill jojoba. Yellowing leaves and mushy basal stems are the first warning signs. The same deep-and-infrequent rhythm that keeps cacti healthy applies here: soak the root zone thoroughly, then let the soil dry completely before watering again. Our guide on how to water cacti and succulents without killing them covers the seasonal adjustments that apply equally to jojoba.

Pruning and Frost Tolerance

Jojoba requires minimal pruning. Remove dead or crossing branches in late winter. Trim lightly in spring to shape a hedge line. Avoid heavy pruning in fall — stimulating new growth before a cold snap invites frost damage.

Jojoba is hardy to approximately 15°F (–9°C) and handles brief freezes without damage in most Sonoran Desert locations. Mature plants in the Tucson and Phoenix areas rarely need frost protection. Young plants in colder microclimates — north-facing slopes, valley bottoms, or higher elevations — may need a frost cloth during their first two winters. Once established, their deep taproot insulates them against all but the hardest freezes.

Harvesting and Using Jojoba Seeds

Female plants produce tan, acorn-shaped seeds from late summer through fall. Gather them once they dry and begin to fall naturally. Spread seeds in a single layer in a warm, ventilated space for several weeks, then store in a sealed jar in a cool, dark location. Unlike cooking oils, jojoba wax does not go rancid and keeps indefinitely.

To extract the wax at home, grind dried seeds in a cold-press oil extractor. The resulting liquid wax is a deep golden color when fresh and solidifies slightly below room temperature. Applied directly to skin or hair, pure jojoba wax absorbs quickly without leaving a greasy residue — and because it's chemically similar to human sebum, it's non-comedogenic and suited to nearly every skin type.

Jojoba is one of the most rewarding native shrubs you can add to a desert landscape — functional, evergreen, wildlife-friendly, and nearly self-sufficient once its roots find water.
