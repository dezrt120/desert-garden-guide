How to Grow Figs in the Desert

June 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by Quang Nguyen Vinh on Pexels

Ficus carica evolved in the same hot, dry, calcium-rich soils of the eastern Mediterranean that share so much climate DNA with the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts. That's not a coincidence — it's an opportunity. Figs are one of the most productive, low-drama fruit trees you can plant in an arid garden, delivering 40 to 60 pounds of fruit per mature tree with surprisingly little water once established. If you've been hesitating because you thought figs were too tropical or too delicate for desert heat, this guide will change your mind.

Why Figs Thrive in the Desert

Most fruit trees struggle in the low desert because they demand cool winters, humid summers, or deep, loamy soil. Figs need none of those things. Ficus carica is native to a climate zone that regularly sees 100°F summers, months without rain, thin rocky soils, and winter lows that occasionally dip below freezing. It survives dry spells by drawing on a deep taproot system, goes dormant in winter cold, and returns vigorously each spring.

In Phoenix, Tucson, El Paso, and similar low- and mid-desert climates, figs can produce two full crops per season: a lighter "breba" crop in early summer on last year's wood, and a heavier main crop in late summer and fall. Begin with a solid foundation — see our beginner's guide to starting a desert garden for soil preparation and microclimate principles that apply equally to fruit trees.

Best Fig Varieties for Desert Gardens

Not every fig performs equally in desert conditions. Heat-split, thin skin, and poor sugar development are common problems with the wrong variety. These five varieties consistently perform well in the desert Southwest:

Brown Turkey and Black Mission are the default recommendation for most desert gardeners because they establish quickly, tolerate harsh sun, and fruit reliably even in years with irregular rainfall.

Planting: Timing and Location

The ideal planting window in the low desert is October through February, when cooler temperatures let the root system establish before summer heat arrives. This is the same sweet spot described in our guide to the best time of year to plant in the desert — and it applies equally to figs.

Choose a south- or east-facing location with at least eight hours of direct sun. Figs produce more fruit in full sun, and the heat helps sugars concentrate. Avoid north-facing walls and spots shaded by buildings in the afternoon — the fruit will be larger but bland.

Figs grown in containers are an excellent option for gardeners with limited space, caliche-heavy soil, or a desire to move the tree indoors during hard freezes. A 25-gallon terracotta or heavy plastic container gives a fig tree room to produce several pounds of fruit while remaining manageable. Container figs need more frequent watering than in-ground trees — check soil moisture every three to four days in peak summer.

Soil Preparation

Figs tolerate poor soil better than almost any other fruit tree, but they strongly dislike waterlogged roots. In heavy clay or caliche soils, dig a wide planting hole — at least three feet across — and break through any hardpan layer beneath. Backfill with native soil amended with coarse sand or crushed granite at a 3:1 ratio. Do not add compost to the backfill; it improves drainage but also promotes excessive leafy growth at the expense of fruit.

If you have severe caliche, consider building a raised mound 12 to 18 inches high and at least four feet wide. Plant the fig on the mound, and the roots will eventually penetrate the native soil below as the caliche breaks down from repeated wetting and drying cycles. The same approach works for other fruit trees — see our guides on growing vegetables in the desert and growing pomegranates in the desert for more on managing difficult desert soils.

Watering Figs in the Desert

New fig trees need more water than established ones, but the rhythm is still deep-and-infrequent rather than shallow-and-frequent — exactly the approach covered in our guide on how to water desert plants without killing them.

For the first two summers, water deeply every seven to ten days. "Deeply" means at least 15 gallons per watering — enough to wet the soil to 18 inches. Set up a drip emitter kit with multiple 2-gallon-per-hour emitters placed at the drip line, not at the trunk. The drip line — the outer edge of the canopy — is where feeder roots concentrate.

Always water the drip line — the outer edge of the canopy — not the trunk. That's where the feeder roots live. Move your emitters outward every year as the canopy expands.

Once established (typically after two full growing seasons), desert figs need far less supplemental water. In Tucson's 12-inch rainfall zone, a mature in-ground fig often needs only two or three deep soaks per month through the hottest months, and nothing at all from November through March. In Phoenix's hotter, drier summers, plan for weekly deep watering from June through September.

Signs of underwatering: leaves curl inward, fruit drops before ripening, and growth stalls. Signs of overwatering: yellowing leaves, root rot at the crown, and split fruit from uneven moisture absorption.

Fertilizing

Figs are light feeders by fruit-tree standards. In desert soils, apply a balanced slow-release fertilizer once in early spring as leaves emerge and once again in early summer after the breba crop is harvested. Avoid high-nitrogen fertilizers in summer — they push vegetative growth at the expense of fruit and increase the risk of frost damage to late-season shoots.

If your tree's leaves are pale yellow-green rather than deep green, a soil test may reveal nitrogen or iron deficiency — both common in alkaline desert soils. Chelated iron applied as a soil drench in spring corrects iron chlorosis within a few weeks.

Pruning Desert Figs

The primary purpose of pruning a desert fig is to establish a strong scaffold structure, maintain manageable size, and maximize fruit production on the right type of wood. Figs are forgiving pruners — mistakes grow back — but a few principles make the job much easier.

  • Prune in late winter (February in the low desert) just as buds swell but before growth begins. Pruning too early risks frost damage to new cuts; pruning too late removes breba crop buds.
  • Remove crossing or inward-growing branches first. The goal is an open vase shape with four to six main scaffold branches radiating outward.
  • Shorten new shoots by one-third to encourage branching and more fruiting spurs along each branch.
  • Never remove more than one-third of the canopy in a single season. Heavy pruning triggers a flush of vegetative growth that delays fruiting by a full year.

Protecting Figs Through Desert Extremes

Summer heat: Established in-ground figs handle full desert sun without protection. Young trees in their first summer may benefit from 30% shade cloth over the west-facing side of the canopy during the hottest weeks. Remove it once monsoon rains begin — the increased humidity and cloud cover dramatically lower heat stress.

Frost: Most desert fig varieties are cold-hardy to 15–20°F, which covers the vast majority of Sonoran Desert locations. The main risk is a hard freeze killing first-year canes. Wrap the trunk with a tree trunk wrap during cold snaps below 25°F, and pile a thick mulch ring at the base to insulate roots. Even if the canopy is killed to the ground by an exceptional freeze, the root system usually survives and sends up new growth in spring.

Birds and wildlife: Figs ripen quickly and attract birds, squirrels, and javelinas. Net the canopy loosely as fruit approaches full size, or harvest slightly underripe and let fruit soften at room temperature. Figs left on the tree past peak ripeness split, ferment, and attract pests rapidly in desert heat.

Harvesting

Figs do not ripen after picking — harvest only when fully ripe. A ripe fig droops slightly on its stem, the skin softens to a gentle give under light pressure, and the neck of the fruit bends rather than standing upright. Color alone is unreliable — Brown Turkey figs can look ripe when they're still firm. Use the feel and droop test.

A healthy mature fig tree in the desert Southwest can produce 40 to 60 pounds of fruit across both crops in a good year. Fresh figs last only two to three days at room temperature; refrigerate for up to a week, or dry, jam, or freeze for longer storage. Dried figs from the desert are often sweeter than commercially dried figs because the low humidity and intense sun concentrate sugars naturally.

Companion Plants for Desert Figs

Figs are generous companions in a desert food garden. Their wide, dense canopy shades the root zone of understory plants that need protection from afternoon sun. Good companions include:

  • Garlic and chives (repel aphids near the trunk)
  • Comfrey (dynamic accumulator; chop-and-drop mulch)
  • Nasturtium (ground cover; deters pests; edible)
  • Desert herbs like rosemary, thyme, and oregano (thrive in the same deep-watering rhythm)

For a broader approach to building productive plant communities in the desert, see our guides to desert companion planting and creating a desert food forest.

Ready to Plant?

Figs reward desert gardeners who respect their one non-negotiable requirement: deep, infrequent water rather than shallow, frequent irrigation. Get that rhythm right, choose a heat-tolerant variety like Brown Turkey or Black Mission, and plant in full sun with well-draining soil — and you'll have a tree that produces generously for decades with minimal effort.
