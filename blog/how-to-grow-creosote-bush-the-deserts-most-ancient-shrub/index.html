How to Grow Creosote Bush: The Desert’s Most Ancient Shrub

July 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by French Sweetie on Pexels

After every desert rain, a distinctive, resinous fragrance rises from the landscape — the scent of creosote bush (Larrea tridentata). It’s one of the most recognizable smells in the American Southwest, and it comes from one of the most remarkable plants on Earth. A single creosote clone in the Mojave Desert — known as King Clone — is estimated to be 11,700 years old, making it one of the oldest living organisms ever discovered.

If you want to grow creosote bush in your garden, you’re working with a plant that has spent millennia perfecting drought survival. Understanding that heritage is the first step to growing it well.

What Is Creosote Bush?

Creosote bush (Larrea tridentata) is the dominant shrub of three North American deserts: the Sonoran, Mojave, and Chihuahuan. It grows from southern California through Nevada and Utah, across Arizona and New Mexico, and into West Texas and Mexico. In many places, it forms vast monocultures — regular spacing across desert flats that looks almost deliberate, a result of each plant releasing chemical inhibitors that prevent competitors from growing too close.

The plant reaches 3–10 feet tall and wide, with small, waxy, olive-green leaves coated in a thick resin that gives it its name. In spring, small bright-yellow flowers appear, followed by fuzzy white seed capsules beloved by birds. The resin acts as a natural sunscreen and water repellent — a survival strategy so effective that the plant can photosynthesize even during droughts that kill everything around it.

Why Plant Creosote Bush in Your Garden?

Creosote bush offers something few other desert shrubs can match: truly extreme, zero-input drought tolerance. Once established, it survives on just 3–5 inches of annual rainfall with no supplemental irrigation at all. That makes it one of the most genuinely water-free plants you can put in a Southwestern landscape.

Beyond water efficiency, creosote supports an extraordinary web of wildlife. Over 60 species of native bees — including the specialist Diadasia australis and Colletes stepheni — depend on creosote flowers for pollen. Harris’s antelope squirrels cache the seeds. Zebra-tailed lizards bask under its canopy. Creosote grasshoppers and creosote katydids evolved to feed exclusively on this plant. If supporting desert biodiversity matters to you, few plants deliver more per gallon of water.

Where Creosote Bush Grows Best

Creosote bush thrives in USDA hardiness zones 7–11. In the Sonoran Desert (Phoenix, Tucson, Yuma), it’s perfectly at home. In the Mojave (Las Vegas, Palm Springs), it’s equally native. In the Chihuahuan Desert (El Paso, Las Cruces, Albuquerque), it handles more cold — surviving brief dips to 0°F in mature specimens.

Full sun is non-negotiable. Creosote bush evolved in the most exposed, sun-baked terrain the continent offers. In partial shade, it grows lanky, sparse, and prone to root rot. Plant it in the hottest, most open corner of your yard with confidence.

Soil must drain freely. Creosote thrives in sandy, rocky, alkaline soils where most plants would fail. Heavy clay and chronically moist areas are the two conditions where it truly struggles. If you’re working with caliche hardpan, break through it before planting so water can drain freely — our guide to starting your desert garden covers soil prep techniques in detail.

When to Plant

Fall is the ideal planting window — September through November in the low Sonoran Desert — when soil temperatures are cooling but still above 50°F. Creosote can establish its root system through winter before facing its first brutal summer. Spring planting (February–March) also works, though the establishment window is shorter before summer heat arrives. How to Plant Creosote Bush

Creosote bush is notoriously difficult to transplant from the wild — the root system is extensive, fragile, and doesn’t survive being dug. Always start with nursery-grown container plants from a native plant nursery.

  1. Dig the hole twice as wide as the container but only as deep. Do not amend the soil — creosote thrives in native, unamended ground.
  2. Position the crown at or slightly above the surrounding grade to ensure water drains away from the stem.
  3. Water deeply at planting, soaking the rootball completely. Build a temporary watering berm around the plant to hold water in place.
  4. Mulch lightly with a 2–3 inch layer of decomposed granite or pea gravel, keeping mulch away from the stem itself.

You can also start creosote from creosote bush seeds, though germination rates are low and the process takes patience. Collect fresh seeds in late spring, lightly scarify them with sandpaper, and sow in sterile sandy mix. Keep barely moist in a warm location — 80–90°F soil temperature. Even successful germination produces a plant that looks modest for 2–3 years before putting on real growth.

Watering During Establishment

This is where most gardeners over-manage creosote. The plant is adapted to drought stress; chronic overwatering is far more dangerous than underwatering.

During the first summer (the establishment year), water every two to three weeks — deeply, to 18–24 inches. By the second year, reduce to once a month. By the third year, you can stop supplemental irrigation entirely in most Sonoran and Mojave locations, relying on monsoon rains and winter precipitation. The same principle applies to many desert natives — our guide to watering drought-adapted desert plants walks through the deep-and-infrequent method in detail.

“Less is always more with creosote bush. A plant that has pushed through a dry summer on minimal water is almost always healthier — and longer-lived — than one that received generous irrigation during its first year.”

A well-placed drip emitter kit makes establishment watering precise and consistent, delivering water slowly to the root zone without wetting the crown or creating standing water.

Companion Planting with Creosote Bush

In nature, creosote typically grows in close association with triangle-leaf bursage (Ambrosia deltoidea) in the Sonoran Desert and white bursage (A. dumosa) in the Mojave. These low, silvery groundcovers stabilize soil, reduce evaporation, and create a natural visual pairing.

In the garden, brittlebush (Encelia farinosa), desert marigold (Baileya multiradiata), globe mallow (Sphaeralcea ambigua), and desert zinnia (Zinnia acerosa) fill the spaces between plants beautifully and provide seasons of color that contrast with creosote’s evergreen structure. Pruning and Long-Term Maintenance

Creosote bush needs almost no maintenance once established. If a dead branch appears, remove it with clean cuts in late winter. Never shear the plant into a mound — heavy pruning stresses the plant, and the dense resinous interior can become a fire hazard in dry conditions.

If a mature plant has grown too large for its space, a hard cutback to 12–18 inches in early spring prompts rapid flushing of new growth. Use clean, sharp bypass pruning shears and wash them after use — the resin is sticky and corrosive to metal over time.

A Note on Toxicity and Traditional Uses

The leaves and resin contain nordihydroguaiaretic acid (NDGA), which is toxic to livestock and dogs in large quantities. Place creosote away from dog runs and pasture areas. For humans, creosote bush has a documented history of medicinal use among Tohono O’odham, Seri, and other Indigenous desert peoples — leaf preparations were used for everything from arthritis to respiratory complaints. Modern research has confirmed potent antioxidant and antimicrobial properties in NDGA, though medical authorities advise caution with dietary supplements at high doses.

The Signature Scent

That famous petrichor-like fragrance after desert rain? It comes from the plant’s resins reacting with water vapor. The smell drifts for miles after the first monsoon of the season — a sensory marker so tied to the Southwest that Arizonans routinely describe it as “home.” Plant creosote near a patio, window, or path, and you’ll catch it every time the sky opens up. No other desert shrub delivers that experience.
