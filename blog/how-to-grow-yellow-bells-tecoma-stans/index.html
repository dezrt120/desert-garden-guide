How to Grow Yellow Bells (Tecoma stans): The Desert's Brightest Native Shrub

July 2026  ·  6 min read

Photo by Bejan Adrian on Pexels

Few desert plants stop people in their tracks quite like Tecoma stans in full bloom. From late spring through the first frost, this sprawling native shrub erupts in clusters of bright yellow, trumpet-shaped flowers that cover the plant so completely you can barely see the leaves. In the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts, yellow bells is a landscape staple — and for good reason. It's fast-growing, intensely drought-tolerant once established, a magnet for hummingbirds and carpenter bees, and one of the longest-blooming shrubs available to Southwestern gardeners.

Despite its showiness, Tecoma stans is surprisingly misunderstood. Many gardeners either overwater it, shear it into an unnatural meatball, or site it somewhere it gets too cold in winter. Get these three things right, and yellow bells rewards you with years of near-effortless color.

What Is Tecoma stans?

Tecoma stans — commonly called yellow bells, yellow trumpet flower, yellow elder, or trumpetbush — is a large, semi-woody shrub in the Bignoniaceae (trumpet vine) family. It's native from the Sonoran Desert through Central and South America, and has naturalized in parts of Florida and Hawaii. In its home territory, it's considered a keystone resource plant: javelinas browse the leaves, hummingbirds drink the nectar, and large carpenter bees (Xylocopa species) buzz through the flowers in the morning.

The species reaches 6–15 feet tall and wide in frost-free areas, though most Tucson or El Paso gardeners see it die back to the crown in cold winters and return from the roots each spring. Several cultivated varieties are available:

In Phoenix (zone 9b–10a), the species and 'Orange Jubilee' behave as true evergreen perennials. In Tucson (zone 9a), expect some dieback after freezes below 26°F — they bounce back reliably from roots. In El Paso or Albuquerque (zone 7b–8b), treat yellow bells as a root-hardy perennial: it dies to the ground but re-sprouts vigorously each spring from a woody base.

Where to Plant Yellow Bells

Tecoma stans demands full sun — at least six hours a day, and preferably eight or more. In partial shade, it grows leggy, produces sparse flowers, and becomes susceptible to fungal diseases. Choose the hottest, sunniest spot in your yard: south-facing walls, west exposures near reflective masonry, or open areas with zero afternoon shade.

The number one mistake with yellow bells is giving them too much shade. They evolved in scrubby, open desert and dry washes — full, unobstructed sun is their natural state. A plant in dappled shade produces maybe 10% of the flowers a sun-grown plant produces.

Good drainage is equally critical. Tecoma stans tolerates virtually any desert soil — caliche, sandy loam, rocky decomposed granite — as long as water doesn't pool around the crown. If you have a low spot with heavy clay, build up the planting area or choose a different site. A plant sitting in wet soil through a cool winter will rot at the crown and fail to re-sprout in spring.

When and How to Plant

In the low desert (Phoenix, Yuma, Tucson), plant yellow bells in spring after the last frost — typically late February through May — or in the fall window between September and mid-November. The fall window is often better: roots establish through the cool season and the plant enters summer with a developed root system ready to support aggressive top growth. To plant:

  1. Dig a hole twice as wide as the container but no deeper than the root ball.
  2. Tease apart any circling roots before setting the plant in place.
  3. Backfill with the native soil — no amendments. Tecoma stans is native to lean, alkaline desert soils and doesn't need or want rich organic compost in the planting hole.
  4. Form a watering basin around the drip line and water thoroughly to settle the soil.
  5. Apply 3–4 inches of decomposed granite mulch out to the drip line, keeping mulch a few inches away from the woody stem.

Watering Schedule

During the first summer, water deeply every 7–10 days to help the root system establish. Run a drip emitter at 2 gallons per hour for 2–3 hours, placing the emitter at the drip line rather than at the trunk. After the first monsoon season — when plants typically put on 2–3 feet of new growth — you can reduce irrigation significantly.

A fully established Tecoma stans (2–3 years in the ground) thrives with almost no supplemental irrigation in the Sonoran Desert. Monthly deep soaks during the dry foresummer (May–June) and monthly soaks during winter dormancy are usually sufficient. During monsoon season, most years you can turn off your drip entirely. Overwatering is the single most common cause of poor flowering and leggy growth — the plant produces more flowers under mild drought stress than it does with generous irrigation.

For a deep dive on managing watering schedules for desert shrubs and natives, our guide on watering desert plants correctly explains the deep-and-infrequent principle that applies equally to Tecoma.

Fertilizing

In alkaline desert soils, yellow bells rarely needs fertilizing and often performs better without it. Excessive nitrogen produces lush, fast-growing foliage at the expense of flowers. If you notice yellowing leaves with green veins (chlorosis from iron or manganese deficiency — common in high-pH soils), apply a chelated iron drench or soil acidifier in early spring. A single application every one to two years is usually sufficient.

Pruning Yellow Bells

This is where most gardeners go wrong. Tecoma stans is a shrub, not a hedge — shearing it into a tight ball destroys its natural graceful arch and removes the new growth where flowers form. Instead, practice structural pruning once a year.

The best time to prune is late winter (February in Tucson, early March in higher-elevation gardens) just before new growth emerges. Cut back the longest, woodiest stems by one-third to one-half, removing dead or crossing branches and opening the center of the plant for air circulation. This rejuvenates the plant and encourages a flush of flower-bearing new growth.

In zones 9a and colder where winter freezes knock back the top growth, let the dead stems stand through winter — they provide some frost protection to the crown — then cut everything dead back to live wood in late February or March when you can see where green resumes.

Mid-Season Deadheading

While not strictly necessary, removing the spent flower clusters and seed pods that form in late summer redirects the plant's energy back into flower production and can extend the bloom season several more weeks. This is especially worth doing on container-grown plants where you want maximum display impact.

Companion Planting

Tecoma stans looks spectacular combined with plants that provide textural contrast. Pair it with the silver-gray foliage of Texas Ranger (Leucophyllum frutescens) for a stunning color combination when both are in bloom. Plant it behind low-growing desert marigold (Baileya multiradiata) to create a layered yellow-on-yellow effect. For a pollinator powerhouse, grow it alongside chuparosa (Justicia californica), whose red tubular flowers overlap with early Tecoma bloom and attract different hummingbird species.

Pests and Problems

Yellow bells is remarkably pest-resistant. In humid conditions, powdery mildew can appear on crowded plants — the solution is always better air circulation, not chemical sprays. In desert conditions, this is rarely a problem.

The primary threats are:

  • Frost damage — in zone 9a and colder, accept that the top growth will die back and plan accordingly. The roots are far hardier than the stems.
  • Root rot — caused by poor drainage or overwatering, especially in winter. Preventable with correct siting.
  • Aphids — occasionally appear on new growth in spring. A strong blast of water or an application of neem oil spray handles them quickly. Ladybugs and parasitic wasps usually take care of aphids naturally if you avoid broad-spectrum pesticides.

Growing Yellow Bells in Containers

The compact variety 'Gold Star' grows beautifully in a large container — 20 gallons or larger — and is a popular choice for covered patios where it can be moved indoors during hard freezes. In containers, use a well-draining cactus and succulent mix, water when the top inch of soil is dry during the growing season, and fertilize lightly with a low-nitrogen bloom-booster fertilizer in spring and midsummer. Container plants need more frequent watering than in-ground plants, but avoid letting them sit in a saucer of water.

Ecological Value

Tecoma stans is one of the most wildlife-valuable plants you can add to a Sonoran or Chihuahuan desert garden. Anna's hummingbirds, Costa's hummingbirds, and Black-chinned hummingbirds visit throughout the day. At dawn, giant carpenter bees (Xylocopa varipuncta) aggressively work every flower cluster — their buzzing is audible from across the yard. Sonoran carpenter bees, bumble bees, and a host of native solitary bees follow throughout the morning.

The seed pods that follow the flowers are eaten by doves and other seed-eating birds, and the dense multi-stemmed structure provides nesting cover for several Sonoran songbird species. For a garden designed from the ground up around pollinators and wildlife, Tecoma stans belongs in the mid-story alongside brittlebush, desert marigold, and salvias.
