Desert Garden Maintenance: A Month-by-Month Calendar

May 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by billow926 on Pexels

The desert doesn't follow a conventional gardening calendar. While gardeners in the Midwest are dreaming of spring, Sonoran desert gardeners are already harvesting cool-season vegetables. While the rest of the country winds down in October, the Chihuahuan and Mojave deserts enter their best planting window of the year. Understanding when to act — and when to leave well enough alone — is the single biggest advantage a desert gardener can have.

This month-by-month guide covers the Sonoran Desert (Arizona, southern California, northwestern Mexico), the Chihuahuan Desert (New Mexico, west Texas, northern Mexico), and the Mojave Desert (southern Nevada, southeastern California). Tasks vary by elevation and microclimate, but the seasonal logic is the same: work with temperature cycles, not against them.

January & February: Assess, Plan, and Protect

These are the coolest months across all three deserts, with hard freezes still possible in January and early February at elevations above 2,000 feet. Your primary jobs are protection and planning.

What to do

  • Inspect for frost damage. Wait until the threat of frost passes before pruning freeze-damaged tissue — the dead material insulates the living plant beneath. Saguaro (Carnegiea gigantea) and organ pipe cactus (Stenocereus thurberi) are especially vulnerable below 28°F.
  • Plant bare-root fruit trees. January is prime time for bareroot citrus, pomegranate, and fig in low-desert zones (USDA 9b–10b). See our low-desert citrus guide for variety selection.
  • Sow cool-season annuals. Snapdragons (Antirrhinum majus), sweet alyssum, and calendula transplant beautifully into prepared beds this month.
  • Sharpen and clean tools. A quality pair of bypass pruning shears maintained with light oil lasts decades and makes clean cuts that heal faster on cacti and shrubs.

Water established natives once every 3–4 weeks if rain hasn't come. Seedlings and newly planted specimens need water every 1–2 weeks even in winter.

March & April: The First Planting Window

Soil temperatures rise above 60°F across most of the low and mid desert by mid-March, and conditions become nearly ideal for establishing new plants before summer heat arrives.

"Plant in fall to root in winter; plant in spring to scramble before summer. Both windows work — missing them both is the only mistake."

What to do

  • Prune frost-damaged tissue. Once nighttime lows stay reliably above 35°F, cut away dead or mushy stems. Use clean cuts just above live tissue and let wounds air-dry before watering.
  • Fertilize desert trees and shrubs. Apply a slow-release, low-phosphorus fertilizer to mesquite (Prosopis spp.), palo verde (Parkinsonia spp.), and desert willow (Chilopsis linearis) once new growth appears. Avoid high-nitrogen products that force soft, heat-vulnerable growth.
  • Plant warm-season annuals from seed. Direct-sow desert marigold (Baileya multiradiata), globe amaranth, and native wildflowers in March for early summer color.
  • Adjust drip irrigation schedules. Move from winter intervals (every 10–14 days for established plants) to spring intervals (every 7–10 days). A programmable drip irrigation timer makes this effortless and prevents both overwatering and drought stress during transitional months.
  • Check for aphids and whitefly. Warm spring temps trigger outbreaks on brittlebush (Encelia farinosa), bougainvillea, and young citrus. A strong blast of water knocks most colonies off without chemicals.

This is also the ideal window for transplanting established desert plants if you need to reconfigure your landscape. Water transplants deeply and mulch the root zone to 3 inches immediately after moving them. May & June: Brace for Summer

Temperatures in the Sonoran Desert routinely hit 105–115°F by June. This is not the time to plant most things — it's the time to protect what's already in the ground and reduce garden stress.

What to do

  • Deep-water mature trees and shrubs. Water infrequently but deeply — 18–24 inches into the root zone. Shallow, frequent watering in summer keeps roots near the scorched soil surface.
  • Mulch aggressively. A 3–4 inch layer of wood-chip mulch around the root zones of trees and shrubs cuts soil temperature by 20°F and reduces evaporation by 50%. Keep mulch 6 inches away from cactus bases to prevent rot.
  • Harvest spring-blooming perennials. Collect seed from penstemon (Penstemon parryi), globe mallow (Sphaeralcea ambigua), and desert marigold for fall sowing.
  • Halt fertilizing. Feeding plants heading into extreme heat forces soft growth that burns and stresses the plant. Stop all fertilization by May 1 in the low desert.
  • Protect young saguaros. Plants under four feet tall may need 30% shade cloth on their west-facing sides during heat events above 108°F. Remove the cloth once temperatures normalize.

July & August: Monsoon Season — Work the Rain

The North American Monsoon delivers 30–50% of the Sonoran and Chihuahuan desert's annual rainfall between early July and mid-September. This is an underused planting window that many gardeners miss entirely.

What to do

  • Plant native perennials and shrubs. Monsoon rains establish root systems better than any irrigation. Desert marigold, chuparosa (Justicia californica), and ruellia thrive when planted just before or during monsoon season.
  • Direct-sow wildflower seeds for fall. Scatter penstemon, desert bluebells (Phacelia campanularia), and Goodding's verbena in cleared areas just before predicted rain. Natural watering triggers strong germination.
  • Check drainage basins. Earthen berms and rainwater swales designed to capture runoff can overflow if not maintained. Clear debris from channels and repack any eroded berms. For a detailed approach to harvesting monsoon water in your garden, earthworks are the highest-value investment you can make.
  • Watch for fungal disease. High humidity combined with heat creates ideal conditions for root rot in cacti and agave. Avoid supplemental irrigation on days when humidity exceeds 50% — the monsoon provides enough.
  • Use a hori-hori garden knife to divide and transplant succulents. Soft monsoon soil makes division easier, and warm nights help cut surfaces callous before cooler fall temperatures arrive.

September & October: The Best Planting Window of the Year

Fall is the single most important planting season in the desert. Soil temperatures remain warm enough for rapid root development, but air temperatures have dropped below 100°F. Plants establish their entire root system before summer heat returns — giving them a two-season head start over spring-planted specimens.

November & December: Wind Down and Prepare

The desert garden slows dramatically after the first hard freeze, but it doesn't stop. November and December are excellent months for infrastructure work and planning.

What to do

  • Apply a final deep watering before freeze season. A thorough soaking in late November helps plants enter dormancy with full root moisture, dramatically improving cold hardiness.
  • Cut back ornamental grasses. Deer grass (Muhlenbergia rigens), pink muhly (M. capillaris), and big sacaton (Sporobolus wrightii) benefit from cutting back to 6–8 inches once they go fully dormant — typically after Thanksgiving in most desert zones.
  • Inventory and replace irrigation emitters. Clogged or cracked emitters from summer mineral buildup reduce water delivery just when plants need consistent moisture most. Walk every drip line before winter and flush the system.
  • Plan next year's layout. Winter is the ideal time to redesign beds, order seeds, and sketch companion plant groupings. Review what worked and what didn't. Think about adding companion plant combinations that reduce water needs through mutual shading and soil improvement.
  • Protect frost-sensitive plants. Cover bougainvillea, citrus, and tropical salvias with frost cloth on nights forecast below 32°F. Remove the cloth by mid-morning the following day to prevent overheating.

The One Rule That Ties It All Together

Every task on this calendar follows a single principle: match your interventions to what the desert is already doing. Water when the desert's natural patterns demand it, plant when soil temperatures align with root development, and rest when summer heat makes establishment impossible. The gardeners who fight those rhythms spend the most time, money, and water on the worst results.
