How to Grow Citrus in the Low Desert

May 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by Soly Moses on Pexels

Growing citrus in the low desert — across the Sonoran Desert regions of Phoenix, Tucson, Yuma, and the broader low-desert Southwest — is one of the most rewarding things an arid-climate gardener can do. Temperatures that would stress a tomato plant in July are exactly what a lemon tree craves. With the right variety, smart planting, and a basic understanding of desert irrigation, you can harvest fresh oranges, lemons, grapefruits, and mandarins from your own backyard year after year.

This guide covers which citrus varieties thrive in the low desert, when and how to plant, how to water and fertilize for heavy harvests, and how to protect your investment during the occasional hard frost.

Why the Low Desert Is Citrus Country

The low desert's long, hot summers and mild winters create near-perfect conditions for citrus. Most varieties need extended warm temperatures and cool nights in fall to develop full sweetness — and the Sonoran Desert delivers that combination reliably every October and November. Annual rainfall in Phoenix averages just 7–8 inches, which is exactly why drip irrigation is the foundation of successful citrus culture here.

USDA Hardiness Zones 9b through 11 cover most of the low-desert Southwest. If you garden in Phoenix (Zone 10a), Tucson (Zone 9b), or the Yuma area (Zone 10b), you're squarely in prime citrus territory.

"The low desert doesn't just tolerate citrus — it produces some of the best citrus in the world. Arizona's navel oranges and grapefruit have been famous since the 1920s, and for good reason."

The Best Citrus Varieties for the Low Desert

Navel Oranges (Citrus sinensis)

The 'Washington Navel' remains the gold standard for the low desert. It ripens November through February — right when cool desert nights develop rich, complex sweetness — and a mature tree can produce 200 or more pounds of fruit per year once established. Plant in a south- or southeast-facing location for maximum warmth. 'Cara Cara' is another standout, producing deep pink-fleshed fruit with a sweeter, lower-acid flavor that ripens alongside Washington Navel.

Lemons (Citrus limon)

'Eureka' and 'Lisbon' lemons produce fruit nearly year-round in the low desert, with primary harvests in fall and winter and smaller flushes in spring and summer. Both tolerate heat and occasional cold snaps better than most citrus, making them the most forgiving choice for beginners. If you add just one citrus tree, start with a lemon — it's the most versatile and the most reliable.

For something more unusual, 'Meyer' lemon (Citrus × meyeri) — a natural cross between a lemon and a mandarin — delivers sweeter, thin-skinned fruit with lower acidity. It's slightly more cold-sensitive, so in Zone 9b Tucson, plant it against a south-facing wall to capture extra warmth.

Grapefruit (Citrus paradisi)

'Rio Red' and 'Oroblanco' are top performers across the low desert. 'Rio Red' produces its signature pink-red flesh between November and March and is among the sweetest grapefruits available. Grapefruit trees are large — they can reach 25 feet — so plan for space or commit to regular canopy pruning. They also need the most heat to ripen fully, making them better suited to Phoenix and Yuma than to the slightly cooler Tucson uplands.

Mandarins (Citrus reticulata)

'Satsuma' mandarins are the most cold-hardy citrus available and thrive throughout Zones 9b–11. They ripen in October and November — the earliest citrus in the desert garden — and the trees stay compact enough for container growing. 'Owari Satsuma' and 'Clementine' are both excellent picks, with easy-peeling skins and intensely sweet flesh.

When and How to Plant Citrus in the Low Desert

The best planting windows are March through April or September through October. Both shoulder seasons let roots establish before extreme heat or cold arrives. Avoid planting in June or July — triple-digit temperatures dramatically reduce survival rates for newly planted trees. For the full seasonal planting calendar, see our guide on the best time of year to plant in the desert.

Site selection: Choose a south- or southeast-facing spot with at least 8 hours of direct sun daily. Citrus planted against a south-facing masonry wall gains a microclimate advantage of 3–5°F — critical during rare hard-frost events.

Soil preparation: Citrus demand excellent drainage above all else. If your yard has a caliche hardpan layer, break through it before planting — citrus roots drown in perched water above impermeable soil. Dig the planting hole twice as wide as the root ball and only as deep. In caliche-prone areas, build a raised mound 18–24 inches high and plant into that mound.

Planting depth: Keep the bud union — the swollen joint near the base of the trunk — 4–6 inches above the soil line. Burying it invites crown rot, one of the most common citrus killers in the desert Southwest. Backfill with native soil only; added organic amendments can create drainage discontinuities that trap moisture.

Start with a 5-gallon citrus tree from a local nursery when possible — acclimated desert-grown plants establish faster and with far less stress than mail-order alternatives.

Watering Citrus in the Desert

Newly planted citrus needs deep, frequent irrigation for the first two growing seasons. Water the root zone — a basin two to three times the diameter of the canopy — deeply two to three times per week during summer, tapering to once per week in winter. The goal is to wet the soil to 24 inches with each watering session, then let it partially dry before irrigating again.

Established citrus (three or more years in the ground) is significantly more drought-tolerant. Deep watering every 7–14 days in summer and every three to four weeks in winter is usually sufficient. The same principle that applies to other desert plants holds here: deep, infrequent watering trains roots to grow downward, building long-term drought resilience. A dedicated drip irrigation system for fruit trees delivers water slowly at the root zone and is the most efficient long-term solution.

Watch for these warning signs: yellowing leaves dropping from the interior canopy indicate overwatering; small, puckered leaves with curled margins indicate underwatering or heat stress. Citrus roots need both moisture and oxygen — soggy soil suffocates them just as surely as drought does.

Fertilizing for Maximum Fruit Production

Citrus are heavy nitrogen feeders. In the low desert, apply a granular citrus fertilizer three times per year: February, May, and August. Use a formulation with a high nitrogen ratio — such as 12-6-6 — and added micronutrients including iron, manganese, and zinc. Desert soils tend to be alkaline, which locks up micronutrients at the root zone. If leaves show interveinal yellowing (chlorosis) in spring, a foliar application of chelated iron corrects the deficiency quickly.

Never fertilize after September. Pushing new vegetative growth in fall creates tender young shoots that a cold snap can kill overnight.

Protecting Citrus from Frost

Low-desert citrus is surprisingly cold-sensitive given the summer heat. Most varieties sustain fruit or foliage damage below 28°F, and young trees can die to the ground at 26°F. When a hard-freeze warning is issued:

  • Cover the canopy with frost cloth or an old bedsheet, draping all the way to the ground to trap radiant heat from the soil.
  • Water deeply the day before — moist soil retains heat far better than dry soil throughout a cold night.
  • String incandescent bulbs through the canopy as a supplemental heat source; LED lights emit almost no warmth.
  • Mulch the base of young trees with 4–6 inches of wood chip mulch to insulate the root zone and lower trunk.

The bud union is the most critical zone. If it freezes, the rootstock may survive but the named variety above the graft is lost. Protecting the lower trunk is just as important as protecting the canopy.

A Rewarding Desert Crop

Few investments in the desert garden pay off like a mature citrus tree. Plant smart in fall or spring, water deeply rather than frequently, feed three times a year, and provide basic frost protection on the handful of nights it matters — and a single tree will reward you with more fresh fruit than you can use for decades.
