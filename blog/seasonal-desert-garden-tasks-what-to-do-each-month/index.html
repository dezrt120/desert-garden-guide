Seasonal Desert Garden Tasks: What to Do Each Month

June 2026  ·  7 min read

Photo by Việt Anh Nguyễn on Pexels

Desert gardening doesn't follow the calendar most of us grew up with. While temperate gardeners think in four neat seasons, the arid Southwest runs on a different rhythm — two planting windows, a brutal summer shutdown, and a monsoon pulse that reshapes everything in July and August. Miss the right window by even two weeks and a plant that should thrive will spend months just trying to survive.

This month-by-month guide gives you a concrete action plan for every task worth doing, from the Sonoran Desert around Tucson and Phoenix to the Chihuahuan Basin in El Paso and Las Cruces, and the high-desert Mojave around Las Vegas. Regional notes are included where the timing diverges meaningfully.

The desert rewards the gardener who works with its calendar, not against it. Plant in the cool shoulder seasons, water deeply and rarely, and let the land rest during the summer furnace.

January & February: Planning Season and Cool-Season Maintenance

These are the most underrated months in the desert garden. Daytime temperatures in the low desert hover between 55–70°F — ideal for planting and for root establishment before summer heat arrives.

What to Do

  • Plant native shrubs and trees now. Brittlebush (Encelia farinosa), desert marigold (Baileya multiradiata), and fairy duster (Calliandra eriophylla) planted in January or February get five months to anchor roots before summer. Check out our guide to desert shade trees if you're adding a canopy layer this year.
  • Sow cool-season vegetables. Broccoli, kale, lettuce, chard, and beets transplant well into low-desert beds now. For detailed timing by crop, see the desert vegetable seasonal guide.
  • Prune frost-damaged growth. Wait until you're past the last freeze date for your zone before cutting back frost-nipped stems on bougainvillea, red bird of paradise (Caesalpinia pulcherrima), or yellow bells (Tecoma stans). Cutting too early exposes tender tissue to one last cold snap.
  • Apply pre-emergent herbicide to gravel areas before soil temps hit 55°F — that's the germination threshold for Sahara mustard and London rocket.
  • Service drip systems. Flush filters, check emitters, and flush main lines before the active growing season begins.

March & April: Prime Planting Window

This is the most important two-month stretch in the desert gardening year. Soil temperatures are warming but air temperatures haven't yet turned punishing. Plants put in the ground now have exactly the root establishment window they need.

What to Do

  • Plant perennials, cacti, and succulents. Agave, saguaro starts, ocotillo, and most native perennials go in now. Anything planted after mid-May will need intensive irrigation support through its first summer.
  • Direct-sow wildflower seeds for late-spring color. Desert marigold, globe mallow (Sphaeralcea ambigua), and black-eyed Susan will bloom by May if seeded in early March.
  • Start warm-season vegetables. Wait until after the last frost date — late February in Phoenix, mid-March in Tucson, late March to early April in Las Cruces and Las Vegas. Tomatoes, peppers, squash, and beans go in now.
  • Fertilize citrus trees with a citrus-specific slow-release fertilizer as bloom sets. This is the critical feeding window for citrus in the low desert — skip it and fruit set suffers.
  • Mulch everything. A 3–4 inch layer of organic or inorganic mulch applied now cuts soil moisture loss dramatically as temperatures climb. See our mulching guide for which materials work best where.
  • Check irrigation scheduling. Bump run times up as days lengthen and temperatures rise. Established desert plants that needed water every 14 days in winter may need it every 7–10 days by late April.

May & June: Race to Summer

The temperature surge from April to June is dramatic — Phoenix routinely hits 105°F by late May, and soil surface temperatures under direct sun can exceed 140°F. The window for comfortable outdoor gardening narrows to early mornings.

What to Do

  • Finish all major planting by May 15. Anything planted after that date will struggle through its first summer without significant additional water. If you missed the window, wait for monsoon or fall.
  • Harvest spring vegetables before they bolt. Lettuce, spinach, and cilantro will bolt quickly once days consistently top 85°F. Harvest promptly and pull plants when they go to seed.
  • Harvest prickly pear tunas. In the Sonoran Desert, the fruit of Opuntia engelmannii ripens to deep magenta in May and June. Use tongs and thick gloves — the glochids are no joke.
  • Deep-water established trees once before the summer begins. A slow, deep soak to 24–36 inches now gives mesquite, palo verde, and desert willow the moisture reserves they need.
  • Stop fertilizing woody plants and succulents. Pushing new growth heading into summer stress is counterproductive — new growth is soft and more vulnerable to heat and pests.
  • Install shade cloth over vegetable beds or frost-sensitive container plants if you're trying to keep them alive through summer. 30–40% shade cloth can drop leaf surface temperatures by 15–20°F.

July & August: Monsoon Season — Work With It

The North American Monsoon typically arrives in the Sonoran Desert between July 4 and July 15, bringing dramatic afternoon thunderstorms and 40–60% of the annual rainfall in just two months. In the Chihuahuan Desert, monsoon rains are more consistent and can be substantial. The Mojave sees much less monsoon activity.

What to Do

  • Reduce or pause irrigation during active monsoon. Cacti and succulents that receive regular monsoon rain do not need supplemental water. Overwatering during monsoon is one of the most common causes of root rot in desert-adapted plants. Review our guide on watering cacti and succulents correctly to calibrate your monsoon schedule.
  • Capture runoff actively. Direct roof runoff into basins around trees and shrubs. Even a modest ½-inch storm on a 1,500 sq ft roof generates nearly 470 gallons of water — free, and better than tap water. See the rainwater harvesting guide for earthwork and barrel options.
  • Plant desert-adapted plants during or after monsoon. This is a hidden second planting window — monsoon-cooled soils and regular rain give new plants an excellent start. Native plants that evolved with monsoon timing actually establish fastest when planted in late July or August.
  • Sow desert wildflower seeds in late August after monsoon breaks. Mexican gold poppy (Eschscholzia mexicana), owl's clover (Castilleja exserta), and Arizona lupine need fall germination to bloom in spring.
  • Watch for monsoon pests. Whiteflies, spider mites, and fungal problems spike in the humidity spike after storms. Inspect the undersides of leaves weekly on tomatoes, peppers, and citrus.

September & October: Fall Planting — The Second Best Window

Fall is the second-most important planting season in the desert, and in some ways it's more forgiving than spring. Soil is still warm enough for quick root establishment, but cooling air temperatures reduce plant stress. Plants put in the ground in September or October will have a full winter and spring to develop roots before their first summer.

This is also the best time to plant bare-root roses, perennials from seed, and any bare-root fruit trees you've ordered. Follow the desert planting timing guide for exact dates by city and plant type.

November & December: Slow Season, Smart Prep

The low desert doesn't freeze hard in November and December — Phoenix averages a low of about 42°F in December, and Tucson around 37°F. But hard freezes are possible, especially at higher elevations and in low-lying frost pockets.

What to Do

  • Cut irrigation run times dramatically. Desert plants in dormancy or near-dormancy need far less water. Many established natives can go 30+ days without supplemental irrigation during cool, cloudy winter months.
  • Plant cool-season annuals like alyssum, snapdragons, and Iceland poppies for winter color. These thrive in low-desert winters and freeze out gracefully in spring.
  • Prepare frost protection materials. Have frost cloth on hand before the first hard freeze warning. Frost cloth rated to 28°F protects most frost-sensitive plants; cover from the top down and anchor the edges. Burlap works in a pinch but breathes better than plastic.
  • Prune only selectively. Resist the urge for a big autumn cleanup — dead stems and leaves on perennials insulate crown tissue during cold snaps. Wait until February for major pruning.
  • Harvest citrus. Navel oranges, grapefruits, and lemons typically ripen November through February in the low desert. Citrus left on the tree through light frosts is generally fine — the peel insulates the fruit — but hard freezes (below 28°F for several hours) can damage both fruit and wood.
  • Plan next year's layout. The quiet months are perfect for sketching a new bed, ordering seeds, or reading up on companion planting strategies like those covered in the desert companion planting guide.

Year-Round Tasks That Never Stop

A few tasks belong on your weekly or monthly checklist regardless of the season:

  • Inspect drip emitters for clogs or displacement — a clogged emitter can silently kill a plant over weeks.
  • Pull weeds when small. Puncturevine (Tribulus terrestris), Sahara mustard (Brassica tournefortii), and buffelgrass (Cenchrus ciliaris) are most vulnerable when they're under 2 inches tall.
  • Check for pest damage on the undersides of leaves — scale, mealybug, and aphids are year-round problems on citrus and ornamentals.
  • Top off mulch as it breaks down or blows away. Maintaining that 3-inch layer is the single highest-return task in any desert garden.

The desert garden rewards attention at the right moments and forgives neglect in between. Master the timing, and you'll spend less time fighting the climate and more time watching your garden thrive.
