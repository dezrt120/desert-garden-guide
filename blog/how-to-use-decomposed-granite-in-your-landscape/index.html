How to Use Decomposed Granite in Your Landscape

May 2026  ·  6 min read

Photo by Francesco Ungaro on Pexels

Walk through almost any well-designed desert yard in Tucson, Phoenix, or Las Cruces and you will likely be standing on decomposed granite. DG — as landscape professionals call it — is the natural byproduct of granite weathering into fine, angular particles. It packs, drains, and looks at home in arid landscapes in a way that imported bark mulch and river rock never quite do. But it is not foolproof. Use the wrong type, skip the edging, or lay it directly over bare weedy soil, and you will spend the next three summers fighting a losing battle against puncturevine and Bermuda grass poking up between the stones.

This guide covers everything you need to install decomposed granite the right way the first time — from choosing your grade to stabilizing the surface for foot traffic.

What Is Decomposed Granite?

Decomposed granite is granite rock that has weathered over thousands of years into a mix of small pebbles, coarse sand, and clay-like fines. Particles range from about ⅜ inch down to powder, and that blend is exactly what gives DG its useful properties: the fines bind the larger particles together so the surface stays put rather than shifting underfoot or washing out in a monsoon.

You will find three main forms at the quarry or landscape supply yard:

  • Loose DG — the straight-from-the-quarry blend, best for planting areas and pathways where you want a naturalistic, slightly crunchy surface.
  • Stabilized DG — loose DG mixed with a polymer binder before it is laid down. It sets almost like soft concrete and resists tracking and washout far better than loose material.
  • Resin-coated DG — the firmest version, used for patios and driveways where stability is paramount.

For most residential desert gardens, loose DG in planting beds and stabilized DG on pathways is the winning combination.

Choosing the Right Color

Decomposed granite comes in several natural colors depending on its mineral source: golden tan (the most common Sonoran Desert palette), warm russet, silver-grey, and a pale pinkish buff. Color choice is partly aesthetic, but it also affects heat absorption. Darker DG in a full-sun space can raise surface temperatures noticeably in July and August. Golden or grey tones reflect more light and keep the area cooler — an important consideration if you are mulching around temperature-sensitive plants or creating a seating area.

Match your DG color to the dominant stone already present in your landscape. A russet gravel path leading to a bed mulched with golden DG looks unsettled. Use one material throughout the hardscape and vary texture with plants instead.

How Much Do You Need?

The standard depth for DG mulch in a planting bed is 2–3 inches. For a pathway or patio surface, 3–4 inches gives you a stable, weed-suppressing layer. Use this formula to estimate your tonnage:

Weed Barrier: To Use or Not?

This is the most debated step in any DG installation. Landscape fabric under DG does slow weed germination — but only for the first two or three seasons. As DG slowly decomposes further, organic matter accumulates on top of the fabric and provides a seedbed for weeds anyway. Meanwhile, the fabric prevents rainwater and irrigation from penetrating to plant roots, blocks soil organisms, and becomes a nightmare to remove later.

A better approach: kill existing vegetation with a non-persistent herbicide or solarization before you install, rake the soil smooth, and skip the fabric in planting beds. In purely hardscape areas (paths, patios, driveways) where you have no plants to feed, a heavy-duty woven landscape fabric can extend the life of stabilized DG before weeds establish.

Step-by-Step Installation

1. Define Your Edges First

DG migrates. Without a physical barrier, it spreads across the sidewalk, into the lawn, and into the street within one monsoon season. Install steel or aluminum landscape edging along all borders before you move a single ton of material. Steel edging 4 inches tall is ideal for most applications — it keeps DG in, grass out, and bends cleanly around curves.

2. Grade and Compact the Subbase

Rake the existing soil to remove debris and create a gentle slope (1–2% grade) away from any structure. Compact the subgrade with a hand tamper or plate compactor rented from a local tool yard. A firm subbase prevents the DG layer from sinking unevenly over time.

3. Lay the DG in Lifts

Dump and spread the DG in two passes rather than one. Apply roughly half the target depth, rake it level, and compact it with the plate compactor or tamp by hand. Then add the second lift, rake, and compact again. This two-lift method produces a more stable surface than dumping everything at once.

4. Stabilize If Needed

For pathways that get regular foot traffic, spray a liquid decomposed granite stabilizer over the final compacted layer and tamp once more while it is still wet. The polymer binder locks the fines together so the surface stays firm and does not track into the house. Let it cure 24–48 hours before heavy use.

5. Water In

Wet the entire surface thoroughly with a hose after installation. This settles the fines, firms the surface, and helps reveal low spots that need a touch of extra material before you call the project done.

DG and Plants: Getting the Relationship Right

Decomposed granite makes excellent mulch around most desert-adapted plants — it reflects radiant heat away from root zones during summer, allows rain to penetrate freely, and dries quickly enough to prevent the crown rot that kills so many cacti and succulents (see our guide on how to water cacti and succulents without killing them for why drainage matters so much). Keep DG pulled back 2–3 inches from the base of each plant rather than mounding it against the stem.

One note of caution: DG can raise soil pH slightly over time as its mineral fines interact with the existing alkaline desert soil. If you are growing pH-sensitive plants like citrus or certain herbs, top-dress with a thin layer of compost annually to counteract this effect.

DG also pairs naturally with the layered planting approach that makes desert landscapes look their best. When you plan your tree canopy, shrub layer, and ground-level accents together — as covered in our beginner's guide to starting a desert garden — use DG as the unifying surface that ties the plant communities together visually.

Monsoon Maintenance

The Sonoran and Chihuahuan monsoon seasons (July–September) are the main stress test for DG. Heavy rainfall can wash fine particles downhill, expose the subbase along path edges, and deposit sediment against structures. After each significant storm, walk the DG areas and:

  • Push migrated material back inside the edging with a stiff broom
  • Fill any washout channels with a shovelful of fresh DG and tamp flat
  • Check edging stakes to confirm they have not heaved or tilted

A well-installed DG surface typically needs a light top-dress of fresh material every 3–5 years. Budget for about half the original quantity to restore color and depth after natural settling and organic decomposition reduce the layer.

Cost Expectations

Bulk DG from a local landscape supply yard runs $35–$75 per ton depending on color, grade, and your region. A 1,000-square-foot planting bed at 3-inch depth needs roughly 4.5 tons, so material cost alone is $160–$340. Delivery adds $50–$150. If you hire a crew for installation, budget $1.00–$2.00 per square foot for labor on top of materials. DIY installation on a weekend saves most of that labor cost — the main tools you need are a wheelbarrow, flat-edged shovel, and a sturdy rake.
