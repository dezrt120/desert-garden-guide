How to Grow Creosote Bush: The Desert's Most Iconic Plant

July 2026  ·  6 min read

Photo by Simon Petereit on Pexels

Step outside after a desert rainstorm and breathe deep. That sharp, resinous scent — earthy, medicinal, unmistakable — comes from Larrea tridentata, the creosote bush. More than any saguaro silhouette or prickly pear paddle, that smell is the desert's true signature. Creosote has blanketed the lowlands of the Sonoran, Mojave, and Chihuahuan deserts for tens of thousands of years, and for good reason: almost nothing survives desert extremes as gracefully or contributes as much to the ecosystem. If you're building a water-wise native plant garden, creosote deserves a starring role.

What Is Creosote Bush?

Creosote bush (Larrea tridentata) is an evergreen shrub native across three North American deserts. In the Sonoran Desert (Arizona, Sonora) and Chihuahuan Desert (New Mexico, Texas, Chihuahua), plants typically grow 4–6 feet tall and wide. In the Mojave (California, Nevada), they can reach 10 feet. All share the same distinctive traits: small paired leaves coated in a yellow-green resin, bright yellow five-petaled flowers, and fuzzy white seed capsules that scatter in the wind.

The plant's longevity is staggering. A creosote "clone ring" in California's Mojave Desert — known as King Clone — is estimated to be more than 11,700 years old, making it one of the oldest living organisms on Earth. Each stem growing in that ring is genetically identical, spreading from a single root system that began germinating when woolly mammoths still roamed the Southwest.

Why Plant Creosote Bush?

Beyond its legendary toughness, creosote earns its place in the landscape through raw ecological value. It provides:

  • Specialist bee habitat — 22 or more native bee species depend on creosote, including the Larrea-specialist Colletes stepheni, which cannot survive without it
  • Bird cover — cactus wrens, curve-billed thrashers, and Verdin nest in the branches; white-crowned sparrows feed on seeds through winter
  • Nurse plant function — the dense shade at creosote's base moderates soil temperature enough for young saguaro seedlings to survive their first critical summers
  • Year-round structure — evergreen and architectural even when not in bloom

On the practical side, established creosote bush tolerates 115°F heat, alkaline and rocky soils, caliche, and rainfall as low as 3 inches annually without supplemental water.

Choosing the Right Site

Creosote evolved on open bajadas — the gently sloping alluvial fans at the base of desert mountain ranges. Give it conditions that mimic this origin:

  • Full sun, all day — this plant does not compromise on light
  • Perfect drainage — standing water after rain is the fastest way to kill it; on flat ground, mound the planting area slightly
  • Plenty of room — plan for a 6×8-foot mature footprint and space plants at least 6 feet apart at the center

"In the wild, each creosote bush maintains a ring of bare ground through allelopathic root chemicals that suppress competition. Give it that room in your garden, and you'll have a plant that practically landscapes itself."

Decomposed granite mulch spread 3 inches deep (kept away from the stem) replicates the natural bajada surface, moderates soil temperature, and suppresses weeds without trapping moisture against the crown.

How to Plant Creosote Bush

Transplanting wild-collected creosote almost never succeeds — the root system is too extensive and sensitive to disturbance. Always start with a nursery-grown container plant or direct-sow seed.

From a Container

  1. Dig a hole twice as wide as the container, exactly the same depth — never deeper.
  2. Gently loosen any circling roots at the base of the root ball.
  3. Backfill with native soil only — no compost or potting mix. Creosote roots need to encounter native soil immediately to establish mycorrhizal relationships.
  4. Apply a 3-inch layer of decomposed granite mulch, pulled 3 inches back from the stem.
  5. Water deeply at planting, then weekly for the first 6 weeks.

The ideal planting window in the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts is late September through November — monsoon heat has passed, but ground temperatures remain warm enough for root growth before winter. In the Mojave, February through early April works well. For a complete breakdown of desert planting seasons, see The Best Time of Year to Plant in the Desert.

From Seed

Creosote seeds carry a waxy coat that prevents germination without scarification. Lightly sand the seed coat with fine sandpaper, or soak in warm water for 24 hours, before sowing. Direct sow in fall, pressing seeds 1/4 inch into moist sandy soil in a sunny spot. Germination takes 30–60 days. Seedlings grow slowly — expect 3–5 years before a plant reaches 2 feet.

Watering: The Less-Is-More Rule

Once established (usually after 2 full growing seasons), creosote bush in the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts needs no supplemental irrigation — the monsoon season provides all it requires. In the Mojave, one deep soak per month in summer keeps plants looking healthy, though they'll survive without it.

During establishment, water deeply and infrequently:

  • Weeks 1–6: Weekly deep soaking (let water penetrate 18 inches)
  • Month 3–12: Every 2–3 weeks in summer, monthly in winter
  • Year 2+: Monthly in summer, nothing in winter
  • Year 3+: Natural rainfall only in most low-desert locations

If you're using low-flow drip irrigation emitters, place a single emitter 12–18 inches from the stem and run cycles longer and less frequently rather than short and daily. For broader guidance on watering desert natives correctly, see How to Water Cacti and Succulents Without Killing Them — the same deep-and-infrequent principle applies here.

Yellowing leaves with a soft, mushy base signal overwatering. Leaf drop outside of extreme heat stress periods signals underwatering — though dropping a portion of leaves in midsummer heat is normal self-protection behavior.

Pruning and Long-Term Care

Creosote is one of the lowest-maintenance shrubs you can grow. It needs no fertilizer, no soil amendment, and minimal pruning. If shaping is desired, remove crossing or dead branches in late winter before the spring bloom flush. Never shear creosote into a rounded hedge — it looks wrong, stresses the plant, and dramatically reduces flowering and bee habitat.

The entire maintenance program for an established plant is:

  1. Refresh the decomposed granite mulch every 2–3 years
  2. Remove dead branches after hard freezes (below 15°F can burn the tips)
  3. Stand back and let it do its thing

Companion Plants for a Creosote Community

In nature, creosote rarely grows alone. The classic Sonoran Desert bajada community pairs it with brittlebush (Encelia farinosa), triangle-leaf bursage (Ambrosia deltoidea), desert marigold (Baileya multiradiata), and spring annual wildflowers that germinate in the bare soil between shrubs. In a garden, these companions create a naturalistic, low-water vignette that looks right because it mirrors the landscape that surrounds it. Learn more about pairing plants the desert way in our desert companion planting guide.

For shade structure above creosote, consider native Southwest shrub seed mixes that include palo verde, ironwood, or desert willow. These canopy trees create the layered habitat structure that birds and pollinators depend on. A Note on Medicinal Use

Indigenous peoples of the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts used creosote bush extensively as a topical antiseptic, antifungal wash, and respiratory remedy. Modern research has confirmed that nordihydroguaiaretic acid (NDGA) — the key resin compound — has genuine antioxidant and antimicrobial properties. However, the FDA has issued warnings against internal use due to documented cases of liver and kidney damage. The safest way to appreciate creosote's chemistry is to wait for rain and breathe deeply.
